## Design System — Leadership Brief  (illustrative, 2026-06-16)

**Bottom line.** The shared UI foundation is solid and actively keeping our interface
consistent, but it's under-documented — which quietly slows new hires and invites
inconsistency in newer areas. The recommended move is a small, time-boxed documentation
investment, not a rebuild.

**Risk.** Most of the rules that keep our product looking consistent are automatically
enforced, so day-to-day risk is low. The exposure is *knowledge*: how and why to use the
foundation lives largely in people's heads and one internal guide. If a key contributor
leaves, that understanding is thin on the ground — and newer parts of the product are already
drifting because the guidance is hard to find.

**Cost.** The leak today is time, not rework: new engineers and designers take longer to
become productive because they reverse-engineer conventions instead of reading them, and
newer areas reinvent UI that already exists. This is recoverable effort, not a sunk cost.

**Velocity.** Where the foundation is well-understood, teams ship fast and consistently. The
documentation gap is the brake on extending that speed to newer teams and to AI-assisted
development, which depends on written, machine-readable conventions.

**Recommended investment.** A focused, ~2–3 week documentation effort on the
highest-traffic, highest-risk parts of the foundation, plus a lightweight log of key
decisions going forward. It buys faster onboarding, less reinvention in new areas, and a
foundation that AI tooling can use reliably — at a fraction of the cost of letting
inconsistency compound.
